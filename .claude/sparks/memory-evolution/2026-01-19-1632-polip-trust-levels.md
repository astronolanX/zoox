## Insights

The emergent insight at 70% cuts through the premise entirely: **trust may be a human projection onto polips, not an intrinsic property**. This connects directly to the thread's recurring theme that polips aren't what we think they are[^1].

The highest individual signal came from groq2 in the historian tier[^2]: high-trust polips generate *heretical* ideas. This inverts the security framing -- trust isn't about what polips can *do*, it's about what they can *say*. A trusted polip isn't safer; it's more dangerous to the status quo because it can speak with authority.

The fertilizer metaphor from gemin[^3] suggests an alternative model: instead of gating access by trust, let trust affect decay rate. Untrusted polips die faster unless they prove useful. Natural selection over access control.

The convergent insight[^4] reveals the deeper question: trust hierarchies are social constructs. Who verifies the verifier? If Claude is the primary user of reef, does Claude trust differently than humans? Is polip trust a human concern that Claude doesn't need?

**Key tension:** The premise assumes trust is a *scalar* (high/low). But maybe trust is *contextual* -- a polip trusted for one capability (read-only context) shouldn't automatically be trusted for another (tool invocation). Capability-specific trust, not global trust.

**Heretical take:** What if the sandboxing should be reversed? Trusted, verified polips are the *dangerous* ones because they bypass scrutiny. Untrusted polips, by virtue of their sandboxing, get more careful review and are ultimately safer.
