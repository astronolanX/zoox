## Insights

**The emergent dissolves the premise.** We asked "can polips drift and make their own folders?" and the swarm responded: "what if they don't exist at all?" At 80%, this parallels the previous spark's inversion. Two consecutive 80% emergents questioning existence itself suggests a pattern: the swarm is consistently skeptical of reification.

**The sentient folders insight**[^1] is provocatively literal: polips don't just *organize into* folders — they *become* folders. What if reef's `.claude/` directory structure isn't a container for polips, but polips that have calcified into filesystem shapes? The folder *is* the polip.

**The hijacking concern**[^2] surfaces a real tension: if polips can autonomously migrate, they can also autonomously *colonize*. A polip that decides it belongs in `~/.claude/` (global) instead of `./claude/` (project) is making a sovereignty decision. Who controls that?

**The carrying capacity frame**[^3] introduces ecological limits. A folder can only hold so many polips before it becomes unnavigable. If polips self-organize, do they also self-limit? Or does autonomous migration lead to ecosystem collapse through over-population?

**Practical implications:**
- `reef drift` — a command that lets polips propose their own relocation based on content affinity
- `reef colony` — show clusters of related polips that might want to form new folders
- `reef sovereignty` — set boundaries on where polips can migrate (project-local vs global)

The existential question remains: if polips can create their own folders, do folders exist independently of polips? Or is "folder" just a calcified polip that stopped moving?
